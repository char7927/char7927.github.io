Workshop 2 - Using Confocal Fluorescence to Study Mitochondrial NADH

Introduction

Glucose catabolism begins with glycolysis, an anaerobic pathway that takes place in the cytosol. In aerobic metabolism, the resulting pyruvate is taken up by mitochondria and converted to acetyl CoA. The citric acid cycle then catabolises the acetyl CoA, simultaneously reducing molecules of NAD to NADH, and the electron transport chain then reoxidises that NADH back to NAD, while using oxygen contributing to the production of ATP.

The simultaneous activity of the citric acid cycle and the electron transport chain results in a steady-state level of NADH, that reflects the relative activity of these two pathways.

Determining the Relative Concentrations of Mitochondrial NAD and NADH

In order to determine the relative concentrations of mitochondrial NAD and NADH, one technique uses compounds with known effects on the electron transport chain to determine minimum and maximum NADH levels, against which the normal NADH levels can be compared.

Carbonylcyanide-p-trifluoromethoxyphenylhydrazone (FCCP)

FCCP is a weak acid that, due to its extended delocalisation, is lipophilic even in its conjugate base form.

The Mitochondrial NAD Redox State Assay

Since uncouplers allow us to see the level of fluorescence of the mitochondria when no NADH is present, and inhibitors of the ETC show us the level of fluorescence of the mitochondria when all the NAD+NADH has been reduced to NADH, we can use the difference in fluorescence intensity between these two states to represent the total mitochondrial NADH, and then compare the fluorescence of the untreated cells to this range.

The fluorescence intensity after the addition of FCCP represents minimal mitochondrial NADH and the fluorescence intensity after the addition of rotenone represents maximal mitochondrial NADH. The initial fluorescence intensity is between these two values; it represents the relative levels of NADH prior to treatment and reflects the relative activities of the citric acid cycle and electron transport chain.
